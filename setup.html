Choosing the Right Tank

For beginners, a 10–20 gallon glass or acrylic tank is ideal. It is big enough to keep the water stable, but small enough to fit in most apartments or bedrooms.

  • Pick a sturdy surface that can support the full weight of the tank.
  • Avoid placing the tank in direct sunlight to reduce algae and temperature swings.
  • Plan access to a power outlet and a sink or bucket for water changes.

Essential Equipment

Your basic freshwater setup should include:

  • A filter rated for your tank size or slightly larger.
  • A heater with an adjustable thermostat (for tropical fish).
  • A thermometer so you can actually monitor the temperature.
  • A water conditioner to remove chlorine and chloramine from tap water.
  • A liquid test kit for ammonia, nitrite, nitrate, and pH.

Understanding the Nitrogen Cycle

The nitrogen cycle is the process where beneficial bacteria convert toxic ammonia from fish waste into less harmful nitrate. Cycling the tank means building up those bacteria before adding a full group of fish.

  1. Set up the tank with substrate, filter, and heater. Add dechlorinated water.
  2. Provide a source of ammonia (fish food, bottled ammonia, or a hardy starter fish).
  3. Test the water every few days for ammonia, nitrite, and nitrate.
  4. Wait until ammonia and nitrite both drop to 0 ppm and nitrate is present before slowly adding more fish.

Cycling can take several weeks, but it prevents burned gills, stress, and early fish deaths.
